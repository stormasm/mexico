One of the aims of this work is to reveal the political-military actions of a stratum of small landowners who concentrated land and weapons during the period of the Revolution, also exercising a certain degree of control over some groups of the rural population, especially in the period between 1880 and 1929.

The Cristero Ladislao Molina has been identified by the researcher Matthew Butler as a figure who moved between liberal practices and various conservative actions, hence his description as a “Cristero liberal.”¹ We could agree with this assessment if we consider his early actions in support of the governorship of Pascual Ortiz Rubio and President Álvaro Obregón, his subsequent participation in the rebellion against Múgica, his involvement in the Delahuertista rebellion, and his incorporation into the Cristero ranks, where, with a very weakened position, he confronted the government of Calles and Governor Lázaro Cárdenas.

A brief review of this trajectory leads us to think of a figure who emerged from the ranks of the Revolution and ended up with the Cristero groups. However, we have found that Molina is not an exceptional case, given that most of the small landowners who became wealthy by purchasing land, both from the subdivided haciendas and from the towns that distributed communal property, share a series of common characteristics that require analysis.

The distribution of communal lands and the subdivision of rentier estates opened up a land market in the Pátzcuaro district during the last two decades of the nineteenth century and the first two of the twentieth. Many of these sales failed to provide buyers with certainty regarding the possession of the acquired property, as they involved plots under dispute within communities or were conducted amidst legal battles among the heirs of subdivided estates.

The ambiguity of boundary lines—or *hijuelas*—and succession disputes over property rights were factors that created opportunities for tenants and sharecroppers to purchase land and expand their holdings across various plots; however, these factors also constrained them, for the preservation of their newly acquired property depended on their ability to guarantee access to land for those who owned none.


The small landowner who emerged under these conditions diverged from the liberal ideal, which envisioned the formation of an industrious and progressive proprietor. Consequently, the *rancheros* who emerged as new political actors employed a significant number of sharecroppers—individuals who gradually became their allies, particularly when some of them decided to take up arms to defend their interests.
